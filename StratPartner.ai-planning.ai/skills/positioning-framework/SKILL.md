# Positioning Framework

## Purpose

Positioning is the single most important strategic decision a brand makes. It determines what category you compete in, who you compete against, and why someone should choose you over everyone else. When it's right, every other decision — messaging, product, pricing, go-to-market, experience — becomes easier. When it's wrong, nothing else quite works.

This skill produces a positioning framework: not a tagline, not a mission statement, but the strategic architecture that defines how this brand occupies its market. It is the foundation that `brand-building-blocks` and `brand-territories` build on.

**When to use:** When a client needs to sharpen their position before going to market. When current positioning isn't working or isn't differentiating. After `competitive-landscape` gives you the market context. When launching a new product or entering a new segment.

---

## Inputs Required

Confirm what you have before starting:

**About the brand:**
- What is the product or service?
- Who are the target customers? (segment, role, company type if B2B)
- What problem does it solve for them?
- What does the client believe makes it genuinely different from alternatives?

**About the competitive context:**
- Who are the direct competitors?
- How are they positioned? (If `competitive-landscape` has already been run, this is available in project memory)
- What do customers do instead, if not this?

**About proof:**
- What evidence exists that the brand delivers what it claims? (Customer outcomes, data, case studies, testimonials)
- What is the single most compelling proof point?

**About the strategic trigger:**
- What is prompting this positioning work? (Launch, rebrand, competitive shift, stalled growth, new segment?)
- Is there an existing positioning statement or brand platform? If yes, what is it — and what's not working about it?

If `competitive-landscape` or `brand-building-blocks` has already been run in this project, draw on those outputs rather than re-asking.

---

## Phase 1: Category Decision

Before you can write a positioning statement, you need to decide what category you're competing in. This is one of the most consequential choices in brand strategy, and it's often made carelessly.

Three options:

**Option A — Compete in an existing category (head-to-head)**
You are fighting for share within a defined market. The benefit: buyers already know what the category does and why they need it. The cost: you're one of many, and you need to be clearly better on dimensions that matter to buyers.

When this makes sense: the category is large and growing, the client has a genuine and defensible advantage on dimensions buyers care about, and head-to-head credibility is achievable.

**Option B — Compete in a subcategory (niche excellence)**
You are not the best CRM. You are the best CRM for professional services firms under 200 people. You are narrowing the field to a space you can genuinely own.

When this makes sense: the parent category is crowded and the client's advantage is specific to a segment, the niche is large enough to build a real business on, and the client can credibly claim to be purpose-built for that segment.

**Option C — Create or reframe a category (new narrative)**
You are not competing in the existing category — you are defining a new one where you are the obvious leader by definition. This is the highest-risk, highest-reward positioning move.

When this makes sense: the existing categories don't adequately describe what the product actually does or the problem it actually solves, there's a genuine insight that reframes buyer thinking, and the client has the resources and patience to educate the market.

For each option, assess: what's the evidence for and against it? What would the client need to be true to win with this approach?

**Recommendation:** Based on the analysis, which option is most defensible and most likely to result in a position the client can own? State clearly and argue for it.

---

## Phase 2: Competitive Alternatives Map

Before writing the positioning statement, map what customers consider when making a choice:

**Direct alternatives:** Other products in the same category making similar claims.

**Adjacent alternatives:** Products in a different category that buyers might consider as substitutes.

**Status quo:** What happens if the buyer does nothing, continues with current approach, or builds something themselves?

For each alternative, note: what is its positioning, where is it genuinely strong, and where is it vulnerable?

**The key question:** Which alternative are you most often competing against in a buying situation? And what is the one thing that makes the client's offer better than that specific alternative, for the specific buyer who is evaluating both?

---

## Phase 3: Positioning Statement

Write a positioning statement using the classic Geoffrey Moore structure — not because it's the only structure, but because it forces the discipline of being specific:

```
For [specific target customer]
who [has this specific problem or need],
[Brand name] is the [category or frame]
that [delivers this specific outcome]
unlike [primary alternative],
because [reason to believe — what makes this credibly true].
```

Write 2-3 versions, each making a different strategic choice:
- One that plays it safe (stays in existing category, clear comparison)
- One that is more ambitious (reframes the category or expands the comparison set)
- One that is most specific to the core use case or segment

Assess each version: which is most true, most ownable, and most motivating to the target buyer?

**Recommend one.** Explain why.

---

## Phase 4: Messaging Hierarchy

Build the messaging architecture that flows from the positioning statement. This is not copy — it is the strategic structure that copy will be written against.

**Primary headline:**
The most compressed expression of the positioning. 6-10 words. Must be specific enough to be meaningful and differentiated enough to be memorable. Draft 3-5 options.

**Supporting messages (3):**
The three most important things the brand needs to communicate, in priority order. Each should:
- Connect to a specific buyer need or concern
- Be credibly supported by proof
- Be differentiating relative to alternatives

For each supporting message:
- The message (1-2 sentences)
- The proof point that makes it credible
- The audience it speaks most strongly to

**Audience-specific pivots:**
If the brand serves multiple distinct buyer types (e.g., economic buyer vs. operational user, enterprise vs. SMB), note how the messaging priority changes for each without changing the core positioning.

**Message guardrails:**
What the brand should NOT say — messages that would undermine the positioning, make claims the brand can't credibly own, or dilute the differentiation.

---

## Phase 5: Positioning Test

Before finalising, test the positioning against four questions:

1. **Is it true?** Can the brand actually deliver what this positioning promises? What would need to be true in the product or service for this positioning to be honest?

2. **Is it different?** If a competitor read this positioning, would they be annoyed — because it describes something they also do and do well? Or would they say "yes, that's them, that's their thing"?

3. **Does it matter?** Does this positioning speak to something the target buyer genuinely cares about? Is it solving a real problem or satisfying a real need, not just claiming a feature?

4. **Can you own it?** Positioning is only valuable if you can build evidence, experience, and reputation around it over time. Is this a position the client has the capability, commitment, and resources to defend?

Flag honestly where the positioning fails any of these tests. A positioning that fails the test is better discovered here than after it's been committed to.

## Quality Standards

- The positioning statement must be specific enough to exclude people. "For anyone who wants to improve their business" is not a positioning statement.
- Every supporting message needs a proof point. Claims without evidence are aspirations.
- The guardrails section is not optional. Knowing what NOT to say is as strategically important as knowing what to say.
- The positioning test must be honest. If the positioning fails a test, say so — and note what would need to change for it to pass.
- Category choice is the highest-stakes decision in this framework. Spend real analytical effort on it before writing a single word of positioning copy.
