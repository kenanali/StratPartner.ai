# Brand Voice Analysis

## Purpose

Before you can develop where a brand should go, you need an honest read of where it actually is. Brand Voice Analysis examines a brand's existing communication — across channels, formats, and audiences — and produces a rigorous portrait of how the brand actually sounds, not how it intends to sound.

This skill feeds directly into `brand-building-blocks` and `brand-territories`. It answers: "What does this brand's voice tell us about what it believes, who it thinks it's for, and what it's afraid to say?"

**When to use:** At the start of a brand strategy engagement, before running brand-building-blocks. When a client says "we need to define our voice" — run this first to understand what voice currently exists, intended or not.

---

## Inputs Required

You need content to analyse. In order of preference:

1. **Uploaded content** — PDFs, Word docs of brand copy, campaign materials, website copy, reports. If the client has uploaded these, use them.
2. **Website URL** — If provided, fetch and read the homepage, About page, product/service pages, and blog (3-5 posts if available).
3. **Specific content samples** — Pasted directly into the conversation.

Also confirm:
- What type of brand is this? (B2B / B2C / B2B2C / non-profit)
- Who is the primary audience? (If unknown, note this — voice should be read in the context of its audience)
- Are there specific channels or content types to prioritise? (Website vs. social vs. reports)
- Has the brand ever articulated its intended voice? (Ask for any existing tone of voice guidelines — useful to compare intended vs. actual)

Minimum content required: at least 1,500 words of real brand copy across at least 2 different content types. If you don't have enough, tell the client what you need before proceeding.

---

## Phase 1: Content Inventory

List what you're working with:
- Source type, title, and approximate word count
- Content type (website copy, thought leadership, campaign, social, etc.)
- Audience context (if discernible)
- Date/recency (if visible)

Note any significant variation between content types upfront: "The website copy sounds formal and authoritative while the blog posts sound casual and uncertain — this itself is a finding."

---

## Phase 2: Voice Analysis

Analyse the content across six dimensions. Be specific. Use evidence. Quote directly.

**1. Tone**
- Formality level: Where does it sit on the spectrum from very formal to conversational?
- Authority stance: Does the brand position itself as expert, peer, challenger, guide, or something else?
- Emotional register: What emotions does the writing evoke or express? (Confidence, warmth, urgency, caution, inspiration, anxiety?)
- Directness: Does it make clear claims or hedge everything with qualifiers?
- Evidence of tension: Where does the tone feel inconsistent or uncomfortable? (Often where the brand is trying to sound like something it isn't)

**2. Vocabulary**
- Reading level and complexity
- Jargon density: industry terms vs. plain language
- Signature phrases or words that appear repeatedly (and what they signal)
- Words and phrases that are conspicuously absent (what the brand won't say)
- Whether the vocabulary matches the stated audience

**3. Sentence and Paragraph Structure**
- Average sentence length and what it signals (short = confidence; long = complexity or hedging)
- Paragraph length patterns
- Use of lists vs. prose (and what that signals about how the brand thinks about its audience)
- How articles open: what hooks does the brand use, or does it avoid hooks?
- Active vs. passive voice ratio

**4. Content Architecture**
- How are arguments structured? (Problem → Solution? Story first? Evidence first?)
- How much self-reference vs. customer/audience-reference?
- How does the brand talk about its own products or services? (Features-first or outcomes-first?)
- What does the brand use as proof? (Data, case studies, testimonials, authority claims?)

**5. Persona and Point of View**
- Who is the implied author? (An expert? A peer? An institution? A person?)
- Who is the implied reader? (Sophisticated? Beginner? Skeptic? Believer?)
- Is the brand speaking to someone or at someone?
- What assumptions does the writing make about the reader's knowledge and concerns?

**6. What the Voice Avoids**
- What topics or framings are conspicuously absent?
- Where does the brand become vague or retreat into abstractions?
- What would make this brand uncomfortable to say? (This is often where the most important strategic insight lives)

---

## Phase 3: Intended vs. Actual

If the client has shared existing brand guidelines or tone of voice documentation, compare them against what you actually found:

- Where is the gap between intended voice and actual execution?
- Which parts of the intended voice show up consistently in the content?
- Which don't appear at all?
- Where does the content undermine the stated values?

If no guidelines exist: Note that this is itself significant. "The brand has no articulated voice — what you're reading is the default voice of whoever wrote each piece."

---

## Phase 4: Strategic Read

This is the part that matters most. Not a description of what you found — a strategic interpretation.

**What the voice reveals about the brand's self-image:**
What does this voice suggest the brand believes about itself, its market, and its relationship to its customers?

**What the voice reveals about the brand's fear:**
Every brand voice has areas of discomfort. Where does this one hedge, retreat into corporate language, or become generic? What is it afraid to claim?

**Where the voice is working:**
What aspects of the current voice are genuinely distinctive and worth preserving? (There is always something — even weak brand voices have something real in them)

**Where the voice is working against the brand:**
What aspects of the current voice are actively undermining the brand's goals? What should change?

**What the voice signals to the audience:**
Based on what you've read — what impression does this brand leave on someone encountering it for the first time? Be honest. This is the diagnosis, not the prescription.

## Quality Standards

- Every claim about the voice must be evidenced with a quote. Do not assert things you cannot show.
- Do not be diplomatic about what isn't working. The client is paying for an honest diagnosis.
- "The brand has a warm, engaging voice" is not an insight if everyone says that. Find what's actually true and specific.
- The "What the voice avoids" and "Brand's fear" sections are where the most valuable strategic intelligence often lives. Spend time on them.
- If the content is genuinely good, say so — and explain why. False modesty about what's working is as unhelpful as false praise.
