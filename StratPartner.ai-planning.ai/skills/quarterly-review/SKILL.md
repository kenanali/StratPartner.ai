# Quarterly Review

## Purpose

A quarterly review for a CX transformation program is not a status update. It's a strategic story told to leadership: here's where we started, here's what we set out to do, here's what actually happened and why, here's what we learned, and here's what we're committing to next quarter.

The difference between a program that loses leadership support and one that builds it is usually in how this story gets told. This skill produces the narrative structure, the evidence architecture, and the framing that makes a CX program review feel like leadership — not like administration.

**When to use:** End of each quarter during a CX transformation program. Before a board presentation or executive review. When the client needs to re-establish momentum or re-earn investment after a difficult quarter.

---

## Inputs Required

Confirm what you have:

**Program context (from project memory — should already be available):**
- Program name and scope
- Key objectives set at the start of this quarter
- Key stakeholders and the audience for this review
- Prior quarter's commitments (what was promised)

**Quarter data (ask client to provide):**
- Metric movements: NPS, CSAT, resolution rate, time-to-value, or whatever metrics the program tracks
- Milestones hit (and missed)
- Key initiatives completed, in progress, or delayed
- Budget status (on track / over / under)
- Team and resource changes
- Key decisions made this quarter
- Biggest blockers encountered

**The honest read:**
- What went better than expected?
- What went worse than expected?
- What would you do differently?
- What's the single most important thing for next quarter?

If prior quarter commitments aren't available in memory, ask the client to describe them before proceeding.

---

## Phase 1: Commitment Accounting

Before anything else, honest accounting of what was committed last quarter and what happened.

For each commitment from last quarter:
- **Delivered:** Met or exceeded
- **Partial:** Partially delivered, with honest explanation
- **Missed:** Not delivered, with honest explanation (not justification)

Do not bury misses in the middle of wins. Accountability up front builds credibility for everything that follows.

---

## Phase 2: Metric Story

Take the quantitative data and tell the story behind the numbers.

**For each key metric:**
- Current value and prior quarter value
- Direction (improving / stable / declining)
- What's driving the movement (not just what moved, but why)
- What the movement means in business terms (not just "NPS went up 4 points" but "4-point NPS improvement in enterprise onboarding represents approximately [n] fewer at-risk renewals")

**The metric narrative:** What does the overall metric picture tell you? Where is the program winning? Where is it lagging? Is the portfolio of metrics moving in a coherent direction, or is there a concerning divergence (e.g., NPS up but churn also up)?

---

## Phase 3: Program Narrative

Tell the strategic story of the quarter in 4-6 paragraphs:

**Opening:** What was this quarter fundamentally about? What was the program trying to prove or establish?

**What happened:** The major moves, milestones, and decisions of the quarter — told as a coherent story, not a list.

**The pivot point or learning:** Every quarter has a moment where something was revealed — a discovery, an obstacle, or a shift in understanding. What was it? What did the program learn?

**Where momentum is building:** What is most genuinely working and why? Be specific about the mechanism — not just "onboarding is improving" but "onboarding is improving because we moved from a ticket-based system to a dedicated implementation manager model."

**Where the work is hard:** Honest acknowledgment of where the program is facing real difficulty. Not complaints — strategic clarity about what's being navigated.

---

## Phase 4: Next Quarter Commitment

Frame next quarter as a set of specific, testable commitments — not aspirations.

**The single most important thing:** If the program could only accomplish one thing next quarter, what should it be and why?

**3-5 specific commitments:** Each commitment should be:
- Specific and measurable (not "improve NPS" but "move enterprise onboarding NPS from 32 to 40")
- Owned (which team or individual is responsible)
- Time-bound (by when within the quarter)
- Honest about dependencies (what needs to be true for this to be achievable)

**What we need from leadership:** Be direct about what the program needs from the executive team to deliver next quarter's commitments. Headcount, decisions, budget, air cover. Programs that don't ask for what they need don't get it.

---

## Phase 5: Risk Register

A brief, honest assessment of the 2-3 risks most likely to derail next quarter's commitments:
- Risk description
- Likelihood (High / Medium / Low)
- Impact if it materialises
- Mitigation already in place

Leadership appreciates the transparency. It also creates the record that protects the program team if a risk materialises.

## Quality Standards

- Commitment accounting must come first and must be honest. A review that buries misses loses the room before it begins.
- The metric story must interpret, not just report. Numbers without narrative are just noise to an executive audience.
- The program narrative must have a point of view. Not "here's what happened" but "here's what this means for where the program is headed."
- Next quarter commitments must be genuinely commitments — specific, measurable, owned. Aspirations dressed as commitments are worse than honest uncertainty.
- "What we need from leadership" must be direct and specific. Vague asks don't get answered.
- The risk register is not optional. Programs that don't surface risks don't get leadership help when risks materialise.
