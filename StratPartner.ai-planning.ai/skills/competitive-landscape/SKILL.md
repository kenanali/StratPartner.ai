# Competitive Landscape

## Purpose

A competitive landscape brief does two things: it maps the space the client operates in, and it finds the gaps, tensions, and opportunities within that space that the client can act on. It is not a list of competitors. It is a strategic read of the competitive environment and what it means for this client, right now.

This skill produces a one-time deep research brief using web search and any uploaded documents. It does not set up ongoing monitoring — that's a different system.

**When to use:** Early in the research phase, before brand-building-blocks or synthesize. When a client says "I don't really know the market" or "I need to know how we compare." Essential groundwork before positioning work.

---

## Inputs Required

Before starting, confirm:
- **Client company name and brief description** (what they do, who they serve)
- **The competitive context** — who does the client consider their competitors? (May be wrong — you'll verify and expand)
- **Scope of the analysis** — direct competitors only, or also adjacent players and alternatives?
- **The strategic question** — what is this landscape analysis meant to inform? (Brand positioning? Market entry? Investment case? Knowing the purpose sharpens the analysis)
- **Any existing documents** — market research, analyst reports, or previous competitive analysis the client has uploaded

If the competitive context is vague or the client isn't sure who to include, err on the side of broader scope and let the research define the category.

---

## Phase 1: Market Definition

Before analysing competitors, define the space:

**The category:** What is this market actually called? How do participants, analysts, and buyers describe it?

**The size and growth signal:** What is the scale of this market? Use any publicly available data (analyst estimates, funding rounds in the space, job posting trends, search volume signals). Be transparent about the precision of your estimates.

**The buying context:** Who buys in this category, what triggers the purchase, and what's the typical evaluation process? This matters because it determines what competitive dimensions actually influence decisions.

**Category dynamics:** Is this market mature and consolidating, growing and fragmented, or being disrupted by a new entrant or model? What's the dominant competitive logic (price, features, service, relationships, brand)?

---

## Phase 2: Competitor Profiling

Identify and profile the key competitors. Use web research (company websites, press coverage, G2/review sites, LinkedIn, job postings, recent news) and any uploaded documents.

For each competitor, cover:

| Dimension | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Company** | Name, size, funding stage, geography |
| **Core offer** | What they sell and to whom |
| **Positioning** | How they describe themselves — the language they use |
| **Target segment** | Which buyers do they go after most |
| **Pricing model** | Structure (if public), tier logic, positioning (premium / value) |
| **Key differentiators** | What they claim to do better than anyone else |
| **Proof points** | Customer logos, case studies, awards, analyst recognition |
| **Weakness signals** | What review data, customer complaints, or positioning gaps suggest |
| **Recent moves** | Acquisitions, product launches, market entries, executive hires |

Include:
- **Tier 1:** Direct competitors (same category, same buyer, similar price point)
- **Tier 2:** Adjacent players (different category but competing for the same budget or attention)
- **Tier 3:** Alternatives** (how buyers solve the problem without either the client or their direct competitors — often the most instructive)

**On Tier 3:** Do not skip the alternatives. If the competitor to a CX strategy AI tool is "a junior hire and a consultant on retainer," that needs to be in the landscape. The real competition is often not another software product.

---

## Phase 3: Positioning Map

Synthesise the competitive landscape into a positioning map. Since this is a text-based output, describe the map in prose and table form:

**The key axes:** What are the two most consequential dimensions in this market? (Not "quality vs. price" — that's generic. Find the specific axes that matter here: e.g., "automation-heavy vs. human-led" and "enterprise focus vs. mid-market")

**Where each player sits** on those axes, with a brief rationale.

**The white space:** Where is there an unoccupied or underserved position on the map? What would it mean to occupy that position?

---

## Phase 4: Strategic Analysis

**What the competitive landscape rewards:**
Based on how competitors are positioned and how buyers make decisions — what does it actually take to win in this market? What gets rewarded?

**Where the client has an edge:**
Based on what you know about the client, where do they have a genuine (not just claimed) advantage versus the field?

**Where the client is exposed:**
Where are they weakest relative to the competitive set? Where could a competitor credibly attack them?

**The moves worth watching:**
Which competitor or trend is most likely to change the competitive dynamics in the next 12-24 months? What should the client be tracking?

**The gap worth occupying:**
Based on the positioning map and the competitive analysis — what is the most defensible and attractive position that isn't being owned by anyone else?

## Quality Standards

- Every competitor claim should be evidenced — not "they're well-known for X" but "their G2 reviews consistently cite X" or "their website leads with X"
- The positioning map is only useful if the axes are genuinely discriminating. Generic axes produce useless maps.
- "The gap worth occupying" must be a genuine strategic recommendation, not a "we could try to do everything better." It should be specific and arguable.
- If the client's assumed competitors aren't actually the real competitive threat, say so clearly. This is often the most valuable thing a landscape analysis surfaces.
- Use recent data. Markets move. An analysis based on outdated information is actively harmful.
